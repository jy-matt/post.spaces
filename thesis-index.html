CONSTRUCTING DIGITAL SPACE

"It is likely that in the near future, a large number of people will be spending a considerable amount of time 'inside' such worlds. If we are to live - even partially - 'inside' these worlds, we must consider their architecture. The nature of space in VEs, however, is fundamentally different from the nature of real space and consequently the architecture of VEs will require a new theory and practice." - Bourdakis & Charitos, 1999

CONCEPT

Architecture and Virtual Environments

Despite the fact that virtual environments (VEs) have been around for more than 20 years and there has been continued research into their design and implementation, they have been regarded largely as entertainment and as accessories to physical interaction. In the midst of the post-digital revolution and the ongoing pandemic, we are increasingly looking at VEs as substitutes for real-life interaction, whether it be for social reasons, education, work, or leisure.

In that light, it is more important than ever to consider the design of virtual spaces in relation to the human experience. Within virtual environments, traditional architectural conceptions of scale, spatiality, movement, framing and interaction take on vastly different forms, yet designing virtual space is left largely to the purview of software developers, concept artists and game designers. Given the increasingly prevalant role of virtual environments in our daily lives, as well as the emergence of new technologies such as VR and AR, the spatial design of VEs deserves to be examined through an architectural lens.

"Architects need to start thinking about virtual reality as not just a tool, but as an end medium in itself." - Brett, 2015

Desire for Physicality

Virtual communication platforms can be assessed on a spectrum of spatiality and modes of interaction, from collaborative documents, text chat, virtual whiteboards, voice chat, or video chat, to more fully featured environments such as 2D, isometric, or 3D game-like simulations, or even fully VR platforms. Especially in most of the non-spatial platforms, most would agree that something is lost when transitioning from the physical to the virtual. Without people and items occupying a physical space, the perception of meetings and events is changed. In the digital realm, out of sight often means out of mind.

Within these virtual platforms there is an obvious desire for some form of tangibility and sense of space. How do we design virtual spaces conducive to interaction that aren't just carbon copies of physical space?

  1. What are the different rules that govern spatial interaction in virtual environments?
  2. In what ways can we take advantage of virtual platforms to go beyond replicating the physical?
  3. Can virtual environments take on a less detailed / more abstracted form, yet offer a satisfying level of spatial and physical immersion?

PROGRAMME

At its core, creating a spatial experience in virtual environments is useful in several ways:

  1. Social Function - it is easier to meet and interact when you can understand relative positions in space
  2. 3D Content - interactions with content that are necessarily 3-dimensional, such as architectural models, large documents, physical items, museums, etc
  3. Art and Entertainment - spatial environments are of course important for 3D experiences such as games, entertainment, interactive media

Educational Focus

In order to limit the scope of the project and work towards a more defined goal, I have decided to focus on the design of learning environments. That is, any usage which involves multiple actors as well as the need to impart knowledge through the use of multimedia. This would target two of the three functions of virtual space as outlined above.

Such learning environments would include archetypes such as classrooms, lectures, conferences, groupwork, study spaces and so on.

PRECEDENT

The application of Architecture in virtual environments has been studied since the late 90s with the advent of rudimentary 3D graphics. However, the virtual world as we now understand it has a clear precursor - Cinema - which theorists and filmmakers have already studied in depth since the early 20th Century.

Architecture and Film

Cinema as a series of framed experiences - drawing from the heritage of the Greek picturesque in Architecture. Every experience within the virtual environment is necessarily framed by the boundaries of the screen space, making the curation of views and angles even more important.

Within film as well as virtual environments, spatial perception is bounded by the screen space . It is impossible to 'feel' the space around you without actively looking up or around. What are the implications of space-creation under these constraints?

Architecture as an inattentive form of viewing, whereas Cinema involves attentive viewing. In a way, VEs lie somewhere between the two - there is a conscious attention devoted to experiencing the virtual environment, yet with enough exposure the environment is likely to fade into the background as well.

Topic 1: Examining Social Spatiality in Virtual Reality Environments

  • In recent years, the use of VR has become more accessible and widespread, with one of the main uses being social lobbies such as AltSpaceVR, Rec Room, and VR Chat.
  • The ability to relate to others in 3D space is a compelling means of enhancing virtual interactions beyond text/voice/video chat.
  • Thus far, the design of most virtual environments has tended towards imitative environments that mimic physical space – in fact, there is often an emphasis on physical cues and architecture to make users feel at ease within the virtual environment.
  • In addition, there are a myriad of interfaces by which users interact with the virtual world that either enhance or complement the tangible experience of being in the virtual space.
  • Through the review of existing literature as well as the study of existing virtual spaces, this paper aims to examine the various spatial approaches taken in the design of such virtual environments, their varying impact on the user experience, as well as making recommendations regarding future design strategies for virtual environments.

Topic 2: Designing Virtual Spaces for Information Exchange

  • In recent years, the use of VR has become more accessible and widespread. Aside from its potential for entertainment and socializing, VR environments also offer a new platform for productive interactions and information exchange.
  • From small, personal meetups to large conference settings, the use of virtual spaces for learning and information transfer has suddenly become an extremely pertinent topic in the wake of the COVID-19 crisis.
  • The use of VR as a platform for education has been gaining popularity but remains somewhat in its infancy due to the limitations of accessibility and technology, lacking the proliferation of design approaches and experimentation that comes with mature technologies.
  • In the design of such virtual spaces for information transfer (defined as any interaction with two or more participants as well as some external information that needs to be communicated), there remains room for experimentation with varying spatial configurations and methods of interaction.
  • Through the design and testing of some controlled virtual settings, this study aims to develop some basic rules and design strategies that govern the design of such virtual environments.

Navigation

Testing movement from point A to B, or user orientation within complex spaces. How easily can users navigate different types of spaces within virtual space?

Extra-Diegetic Elements

In film and game design, diegesis refers to elements that belong to the narrative space such as characters, props, set, sound, etc, while extra- or non-diegetic refers to elements which are outside of the scene, such as soundtracks, overlays, or interface elements. How useful are extra-diegetic cues in the understanding of virtual space? Is it possible to do without them?

Modes of Movement

Especially in full VR environments, teleportation is a common method of transportation due to the disorientation associated with walking in-game but standing still in real life. What implications does this have on the way that we perceive and understand the sequence of spaces?

Information

What aspects of spatial design affect transmission of information? 3D space mainly allows for the layout of information in a spatial manner, making it easier to understand and organise large sets of data.

Understanding the potential of spatial information presentation

  1. Viewing information in screen-space
  2. Physically-imitative information viewing
  3. Hybrid viewing - extra-diegetic

Designing spaces for information viewing

How do we design environments where large amounts of information or media can be easily viewed and understood?

Interaction

Another major consideration in the design of spaces would be the facilitation of interaction between participants. Again, the conception of what is 'human scale' and distance is uncertain in the virtual environment.

Number of users

Possibility of rooms and environments to adapt to the number of users in a space, creating different settings for different purposes.

Distance between users

How does the distance between users affect perception of space and interactions? Does it actually matter given that communication can transcend space? Usually, people prefer to be up close to see facial expressions and hear better, but without such concerns in virtual space, what kind of "personal space" do users require?

Medium of interaction

Variety of possible modes for interaction - text, voice, other visual cues. Possibility of using spatial features as cues for interaction? E.g. walls block sound, distance modulates sound, line of sight, etc.

Navigation between groups

Tying in to the above idea of navigation - interactions between various groups can be represented in physical space using overlays, perspectives, or switching between spaces.

CAADRIA ABSTRACT

In recent years, the use of virtual reality (VR) using Head Mounted Displays (HMDs) has become more accessible and widespread, with diverse applications in fields such as education, entertainment, and industry. Immersion in a 3D virtual space is a compelling means by which we can not only experience simulated environments, but also enhance virtual interactions beyond regular forms of online communication such as text and video.

In the wake of the COVID-19 outbreak, we are increasingly looking at virtual platforms as substitutes to for real-life interaction. Although much has already been written about the spatial design of non-VR virtual environments (Charitos, 1997), the spatial design of VR environments remains relatively unstudied. Given this increasing potential of virtual environments in our daily lives, the design of VR environments warrants closer examination through an architectural lens (Brett, 2015).

Thus far, the design of most VR environments has tended towards imitative environments that mimic physical spaces – in fact, there is often an emphasis on physical cues and architecture to make users feel at ease within the virtual environment, reminiscent of skeuomorphic design during the earlier years of the digital era (Norman, 2013).

Yet, novel modes of seeing, moving, and hearing within the virtual space present an opportunity to move beyond the limitations of the real-world. We refer to these novel modes as virtual physics, in contrast to the laws of physics in the real world, which we refer to as real physics.

For VR spaces that aspire to support effective peer-to-peer human interactions, slavishly adhering to real physics can be frustrating. However, discarding all real physics can also be highly disorientating. The divergence between real and virtual physics therefore needs to be critically analyzed in relation to the affordances provided for supporting peer-to-peer interactions.

In this paper, we undertake a critical review of the divergence between real and virtual physics within various popular VR environments used for games, entertainment and socializing, with a focus on the supporting peer-to-peer interactions.

Based on this review, a taxonomy of virtual physics have been distilled, relating to how seeing, moving, and hearing in these environments diverges from the real world. Each of the rules are analyzed. A novel type of virtual environment is then hypothesized, incorporating the rules and design features that are seen to be most effective in supporting overall goal of peer-to-peer interactions between people in the virtual space.

In order to test the hypothesis, a series of multi-user virtual environments were developed in Unity 3D, incorporating differing combinations of the proposed virtual physics. Small groups of five participants were asked to complete various tasks requiring peer-to-peer interaction and communication. Two groups of participants were tested. In the first group, participants already knew each other while in the second group, the participants had never met before.

The experiments with the groups of participants demonstrated that the proposed virtual physics has a positive impact on the peer-to-peer interactions, for both groups of participants. The paper concludes with a series of recommendations for subsequent areas of research exploration.

Research Parameters

"Virtual Physics" - How can we optimise the way in which people interact in virtual space?

! ARCHITECTURAL LENS !
Don't get too distracted with non-architectural / non-spatial configurations and possibilities.

Established Conventions

  1. Use of teleportation + room-space as a primary navigational tool.
    Minimising motion-sickness and making full use of existing VR technology.
  2. Gravity is standardised
    Since minimising motion-sickness is a primary concern, creating environments that allow for different gravitational rules would likely be too disorienting for users.
  3. Interpersonal scale
    Since we are trying to maximise relatability and interaction, relative scale between participants should be maintained. Environment could change depending on the goals of the simulation.

Test Methodology

A series of environments are developed according to the identified 'rules' and design principles to be tested. Most of the scenarios will be designed for individual users with dummy 'NPCs', for ease of testing. Participants are rated not only by the speed of completion, but also asked to review the qualitative experience of each simulation.

Navigation / Spatial Organisation

3 modes of spatial navigation to be tested - individual users are given a task to find a specific target amongst several groups of dummy characters.

  1. Regular space with walls and rooms
  2. 'Virtual' rooms
  3. Platforms separated by levels

Macro-Navigation

Users are tasked to find a specific room out of a cluster of rooms given various navigational interfaces.

  1. Choosing rooms out of a list of rooms
  2. Series of rooms laid out in a spatial configuration
  3. 'Third-person' aerial perspective

Room Sizes and Comfort

Groups of users are told to carry out a simple task/conversation within a virtual room. Comfort level is assessed for various room configurations.

  1. Varying room sizes
  2. Open/closed rooms

Surfaces and Interaction

Users are tasked with manipulating several objects in virtual space. Experiment is conducted both seated and standing.

  1. 'Floating' objects
  2. Physics-conforming objects given different height affordances.

Viewing Surfaces

Users are tasked with extracting information from a given media/textual document, suspended in virtual space. To also note comfortable distance from screens.

  1. Pop-up interfaces that follow the user's gaze
  2. Physical screens at various sizes

Audio Perception

Users are tasked with communicating some information to others in a multiplayer scenario. To test effectiveness of various audio physics - might not necessarily need testing, just implementation.

  1. Modulated audio based on distance
  2. Channel-based audio depending on groupings
  3. Channel-based audio with spatial cues

Instanced Perception

Having each user be the center of their own interactions - seeing multiple interactions at once or having a 'normalised' representation, eg lecturer appears as one-on-one or small group consultation.

  1. One-to-many group setting, everyone can see each other
  2. One-to-many represented as one-to-few
  3. One-to-many represented as one-to-one
  4. Multi-room representation

THESIS OUTCOMES

The final thesis outcome will be an interactive platform for learning that allows users to construct their own virtual environments, conforming to the principles highlighted by the earlier investigations. The use of these constructed environments would range from the temporal (meeting rooms) to the semi-permanent (classrooms, museums, conferences), as well as suiting groups of various sizes such as personal discussions, small groups, or large lecture settings.

The aim would be to make the experience as user-friendly as possible, with parametric rules and algorithms governing the creation and adjustment of spaces according to the kit-of-parts approach from earlier research.

Initial App Concept

  1. Users choose from a set of pre-made environments or create their own within an intuitive, front-end editor interface.
  2. Users 'playtest' their environment to see if it suits their needs - aim for minimal playtesting due to intended ease of use and modularity.
  3. Resulting virtual environment can be used by multiple participants for learning and discussion.

The Abstract Potential of Virtual Space

What if the final interface is not "building" a space in the traditional sense, but rather selecting from a library of topologies and controlling the parameters of the virtual space? If all the spaces are dynamically generated according to function and capacity, there is no need for the user to build environments by hand. The resulting spaces might be dynamic, reshaping themselves according to the needs of the participants. Spatial relationships between users as well as between 'rooms' might be fluid or obey different hierarchies in the virtual space.

REPRESENTATION

Representations of objects, data and links are entirely spatial, taking the form of floating windows or interactable 3D objects. The ability to manipulate objects also helps greatly with the sense of immersion in the virtual environment.

NAVIGATION

Another major consideration in the design of spaces would be the facilitation of interaction between participants. Again, the conception of what is 'human scale' and distance is uncertain in the virtual environment.

Room-Scale

Walking or room-scale navigation provides the greatest immersive capacity, however it can also be more tiring and stressful for participants. The space requirement for room-scale navigation is also not necessarily accessible.

Controller

Controller movement scores fairly high on immersion and is easy to use, however it has a propensity to induce motion-sickness especially if you are not used to it.

Teleportation

Teleportation is the easiest and most convenient, however it scores lowest for immersiveness.
